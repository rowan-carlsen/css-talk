1846

The first baseball game is played using the now-standard style of play. The game has its roots in centuries-old English sports, but teams have been organizing and growing popular within the US since the 1830s. The "Knickerbocker Rules," standardized in 1845, win out over rival rule systems to form the basis of the game of baseball, and are first used in this 1846 game.

1876

The National League of Professional Baseball Clubs forms under William Hulbert's leadership. Individual professional teams have existed since 1869, but by turning teams into franchises owned and operated by third parties—rather than the players, themselves—the National League turns the game of baseball into a business in which the interests of owners and players conflict.

Of particular importance, the National League introduces the Reserve System, in which teams can prevent each other from bidding on their best players. This eliminates players' ability to seek the best pay for their skills and potentially binds them to the same team for life. While the league and team owners reap huge profits from this, players resent its imposed limits to their salary potential and the forced adoption of a standard player contract. When the American League emerges, it collaborates with the National League to preserve the system and seek ways to increase profits, including via the creation of the World Series.

1913

The Federal League of Professional Baseball Clubs forms after another group of wealthy entrepreneurs attempts to enter the now-lucrative baseball business. The Federal League rejects the reserve clause, offers long-term contracts, and sets up franchises in Eastern cities. Eighteen major league players defect to the Federal League. A year later, the American League sues defecting player Hal Chase for violation of contract. In American League vs. Hal Chase, the New York State Supreme Court rules: “If a baseball player desires to be employed at work for which he is qualified, he must submit to dominion over his personal freedom and control of his services for sale, transfer or exchange without his consent, or abandon his vocation and seek employment at some other kind of labor.” This ruling grants the Reserve Clause legal precedence, a major victory for owners.

1968

The Major League Baseball Players Association hires Marvin Miller, an economist for the powerful and militant US Steel Workers union. Miller convinces players to work in unison despite lawsuits and personal attacks, turning the Association into a worker's union. Under his leadership, this union negotiates the first collective bargaining agreement (CBA) in professional sports, including an increase in minimum salaries, a meal allowance for players, a spring training housing allowance, and a formalized grievance process for players to voice complaints about their team's management to the League. Eight years later, the MLB eliminates the reserve clause, allowing salaries to rise even further. Team owners, however, begin to lose money.

1994

Tension between players and owners leads to a strike at the World Series. The World Series is canceled for the first time since its inception in 1904. The MLB becomes the first-ever major American professional sports league to lose an entire postseason due to labor struggles. The strike ends when the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York issues a preliminary injunction against the team owners. Following the strike, baseball game attendance and TV viewership drops significantly as fans view the strike as evidence of greed by both players and owners; attendance doesn't recover for a decade.

2000

The National League and American League are dissolved as legal entities, with Major League Baseball taking full control.
